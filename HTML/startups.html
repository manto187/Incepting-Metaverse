Quirky - The Crowdsourcing Failure

Category: E-commerce | Failed: 2015 | Funding: $185M

What Was Quirky?

Quirky was a crowdsourced invention platform that allowed community members to submit product ideas, vote on others' ideas, and collaborate on product development. Successful products would be manufactured and sold with revenue shared among contributors.

Why It Failed

  • Poor product selection: Many products had limited market appeal
  • High overhead costs: Manufacturing and inventory costs were unsustainable
  • Revenue sharing complexity: The compensation model was difficult to manage
  • Quality control issues: Some products had technical problems

Key Takeaways

  • Crowdsourcing works better for ideation than full product development
  • Physical products have much higher costs than digital ones
  • Community platforms need clear, sustainable monetization
  • Not all user-generated content translates to viable products

Vine - Acquired Then Shut Down

Category: Social Media | Failed: 2016 | Acquired by: Twitter

What Was Vine?

Vine was a short-form video hosting service where users could share six-second-long looping video clips. It gained massive popularity for its creative constraints and was acquired by Twitter before launch.

Why It Failed

  • Lack of monetization: Twitter failed to implement effective revenue streams
  • Competition: Instagram added video features that surpassed Vine
  • Creator exodus: Top creators left due to lack of earning potential
  • Corporate neglect: Twitter didn't invest in needed features and improvements

Key Takeaways

  • Acquisition doesn't guarantee success - corporate parents can smother innovation
  • Platforms must provide ways for creators to monetize
  • Feature limitations can be strengths but also constraints
  • First-mover advantage disappears without continuous innovation

Sprig - The On-Demand Meal Failure

Category: Food Delivery | Failed: 2017 | Funding: $56M

What Was Sprig?

Sprig was an on-demand food delivery service that offered healthy, chef-prepared meals delivered in under 20 minutes. It operated its own kitchens and delivery fleet to control quality and speed.

Why It Failed

  • High operational costs: Running kitchens and delivery was expensive
  • Limited menu: Few options led to customer fatigue
  • Pricing pressure: Couldn't compete with cheaper alternatives
  • Complex logistics: Food preparation and delivery was hard to scale

Key Takeaways

  • Vertical integration adds costs that may outweigh benefits
  • Food businesses have notoriously thin margins
  • On-demand services often underestimate operational complexity
  • Customer retention requires constant menu innovation
